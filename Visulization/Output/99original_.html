A caspase-like Gene decoy molecule enhances the activity of a paralogous caspase Gene in the yellow fever mosquito, Aedes aegypti. Caspases are cysteine proteases that play critical roles in apoptosis and other key cellular processes. A mechanism of caspase Gene regulation that has been described in mammals and nematodes involves caspase Gene -like decoy molecules, enzymatically inactive caspase Gene homologs that have arisen by gene duplication and acquired the ability to regulate other caspases. Caspase Gene -like decoy molecules are not found in Drosophila melanogaster, raising the question of whether this type of caspase Gene regulation exists in insects. Phylogenomic analysis of caspase Gene genes from twelve Drosophila and three mosquito species revealed several examples of duplicated caspase Gene homologs lacking critical catalytic residues, making them candidate caspase Gene -like decoy molecules. One of these, CASPS18 Gene from the mosquito Aedes aegypti, is a homolog of the D. melanogaster caspase Gene Decay and contains substitutions in two critical amino acid positions, including the catalytic cysteine residue. As expected, CASPS18 Gene lacked caspase Gene activity, but co-expression of CASPS18 Gene with a paralogous caspase Gene , CASPS19 Gene , in mosquito cells or co-incubation of CASPS18 Gene and CASPS19 Gene recombinant proteins resulted in greatly enhanced CASPS19 Gene activity. The discovery of potential caspase Gene -like decoy molecules in several insect species opens new avenues for investigating caspase Gene regulation in insects, particularly in disease vectors such as mosquitoes.
